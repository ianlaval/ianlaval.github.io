(This manual is not intended as complete instructions on sailing the vessel at sea. It is assumed that the operator of the vessel is generally competent in the practice of sailing).

RCD Category:

D 

NB: Lydia B has been marked CE category D solely to meet the minimum legal requirements for entry to the European Union following her passage from the Pacific Northwest to the UK. Please refer to the vessel's Pacific and Atlantic voyaging history, and the extensive history of sister boats, for evidence of its safe ocean-going capability. 

The current owner objected to paying the several thousand pounds sterling required for a higher category under the RCD while boats already within the EU pre-1998 are arbitrarily exempt. Under the European Recreational Craft Directive (RCD) the placing of a vessel in any particular category in no way restricts the vessel to any inshore or offshore area. 

Lydia B may be sailed and sold in the EU on the same legal basis as any other vessel.

NB: Port and starboard water tanks are connected by a valve in the bilge. When the valve is in the "both" position, both water tanks may be filled from either of the two deck fillers. Depending on requirements the tanks may be used separately or together. It is recommended that on long passages where water conservation is important, one tank should be in use and the other closed. The choice of tank may depend on which tack the boat is on. If very long tacks are envisaged (as in Trade Wind passages), the tank in use should be the lee tank in order to reduce the boat's angle of heel.

Risk of flooding.

Maximum recommended load:

1,700kg. Weight should be as evenly distributed as possible. Where practicable, heavier items of stores and extra equipment should be nearer the centre, midships and bottom of the boat. While the heaviest items are water and fuel and these are evenly distributed on or around the centre-line, it should be borne in mind that the batteries are on the starboard side towards the after end. 

Hull openings:

Those operating the boat should be familiar with the position of all seacocks. All seacocks should be inspected at regular intervals. Handles should be operated regularly in order to maintain them in working condition. It is recommended that they should be dismantled, cleaned and greased annually when the boat is out of the water. It is recommended that wooden bungs of the appropriate size be kept ready for use if needed.
The toilet is fitted with a sea-water valve. Except for flushing, this valve should remain closed.
It is recommended that all valves should be closed if the boat is to be left unattended for long periods of time.
Cockpit drains in the forward corners of the cockpit well must be kept clear of debris at all times. The cockpit floor beneath the teak grilles should be maintained clean to prevent drain blockages.
The stuffing box, accessed through the floor of the cockpit locker, should be regularly inspected and tightened and/or repacked when necessary.
Portlights and hatches should be kept closed when the boat is at sea in all but the quietest of weather. It is recommended that the companionway washboards be stowed in a position where they are immediately available to drop into position when necessary. It is not necessary to remove the companionway doors.

Bilge pumps:

The 12v diaphragm bilge (mounted forward in the cockpit locker) is wired to a float switch at the after end of the bilge and to an on-off-automatic switch adjacent to the panel by the navigation desk. This switch should be kept in the "automatic" position, especially when the boat is left unattended. In the "on" position the pump may operated manually.
The Henderson MkV manual bilge pump is mounted in the starboard side of the cockpit well with a water-tight mount for the handle. It is recommended that the handle should be kept readily available to use in the small locker on the starboard side of the cockpit coaming.
A 14,000 litres-per-hour centrifugal bilge pump is mounted in the bilge. This pump may be hard-wired to the batteries for emergency use.
NB: Though a strainer is fitted in the bilge line, it is important to keep the bilge clear of debris at all times so that bilge pipes are not blocked.

Flooding prevention:

 

Risk of Fire:

The operator should consult the manufacturer's handbook for safe operation of the engine.

The engine should be visually checked before starting. Oil and cooling water levels should be checked. Belts should be checked for tightness. Fuel and water pipes should be checked for leaks. The engine seat-water strainer should be checked for blockage. On starting, the flow of cooling sea-water should be confirmed at the stern exhaust outlet. The battery digital monitor should be checked to ensure that the engine alternator is functioning satisfactorily. While the engine is running it is recommended that items should not be stored on top of the engine-hatch cover in order not to accumulate heat. An engine over-heating buzzer is provided in the cockpit array.
Combustible materials should be kept clear of the engine at all times.
Beware of the danger of moving parts, especially drive belts, when the engine is running.

Care should be taken against spillage when filling the diesel tank via the deck filler. It is recommended that the adjacent scupper should be blocked with an oil-absorbing pad when filling is in progress. A no-smoking rule should be observed.

Electrical system:

An isolating switch is provided on the battery compartment. The system may be shut off by switching to the "off" position. Postion "1" switches on the house batteries and position "2" switches on the starter battery. Position "All" switches on both the starter battery and the house batteries. A digital charging controller (Echo-charge) is also fitted. This device ensures that when the engine is running the alternator output first goes to the starter battery to re-charge it, then automatically switches to the house battery when it detects that the starter battery is full. The device also protects the alternator from diode damage whenever the electrical system is shut off with the engine still running.

Charging is provided by four sources:
1. Solar panel. This has its own regulator and requires no adjustment.
2. Wind generator. This has its own regulator and requires no adjustment.
3. Engine alternator. Output is regulated by a three-stage regulator mounted to port in the engine compartment. Consult the manufacturer's handbook for voltage and time settings (dependent on requirements and operating temperatures etc).
4. Shorepower charger. Plugs into 110v outlet in quarterberth bulkhead.

Shorepower: There are seven 110v outlets. These may be connected by either the provided 110V yellow cable with connectors and adaptors, or by the 240v - 110V transformer, depending on where the boat is operating.

Battery electrolyte should be regularly checked. Naked flame should not be allowed near the batteries when they are being filled.
The digital e-meter should be checked frequently to monitor battery performance. House batteries should not be drawn down to less than 50 percent of capacity (preferably not less than 65 percent) to maintain their working life.
When removing a battery, first disconnect the positive connection, followed by the negative. When replacing batteries, ensure that the battery boxes are clean and dry, that the lids are in place and that the security bar and strap are securely fixed.
The battery-box ventilation grilles should be kept clear at all times.
The electrical system is protected by a 250-amp fuse, located in the battery box at the front top. The electrical system must be switched off during the changing of the fuse.

Gas system:

Remember that propane and butane gases are heavier than air and collect at the lowest point on the boat, presenting the most serious explosion hazard. Scrupulous attention to use of gas and all combustible materials is vital aboard the boat at all times. Fire is one of the chief hazards aboard a boat.

An isolated gas locker is provided in the stern. Propane/butane bottles should be stored in this locker and nowhere else on the boat. The locker is vented by a drain hole at the bottom. This hole should be checked to make sure it is always clear.
The gas cooker in the galley is the only device connected to the gas bottle. Gas pipe runs should be machine-crimped at their ends. Pipe runs should be continuous, unjointed lengths of appropriate gas hose between the regulator and the cooker.
The appropriate regulator must be used for either propane or butane supply.
The gas system is controlled by a 12v solenoid, situated in the gas locker. The solenoid is activated a control panel situated to the rear of the galley area. If 12v power fails or is deliberately switched off, the solenoid will close and shut off the gas supply.
A gas detector is mounted at floor level at the foot of the engine hatch, next to the cooker. If the detector detects an accumulation of propane or butane -- and many other combustible gases  -- a loud warning buzzer will sound and the gas supply will be shut down by the solenoid. The system should be checked before the supply is switched on again. See manufacturer's instructions.

Volatile flammable materials should not be stored near the cooker or in the bilge.

Saloon heater:

The saloon heater operates on diesel. It is supplied from a diesel fuel tank house in the cockpit locker. Consult the manufacturer's operating and safety instructions. Spare fuel should be stored only in designated diesel containers and kept away from naked flame.
The heater fuel line runs through the bilge to a filter combined with an on/off switch at the forward end. A fan is fitted to the heater for use in windy conditions.

Fire-fighting equipment:

Two 2.25kg dry-powder fire extinguishers are provided. One is housed in the saloon by the galley bulkhead, port side, and one on the forepeak bulkhead, starboard side.

Means of fire escape:

Escape may be made via the forehatch over the forepeak, the main hatch over the saloon, and by the companionway doors.

Boat handling:

The maximum engine power should not exceed 25.74kw

Consult the engine manufacturer's handbook before starting the engine. Check that docks lines and shorepower connections are free before moving off the dock. 

Emergency steering device:

An emergency tiller is provided in case of wheel steering failure. It is kept in the hanging locker in the saloon and is fitted by removing the bronze deck plate aft in the cockpit. A key is stored in the lower companionway locker.

Safe operation:

Lifeline gates should be kept closed when the boat is at sea. Personal flotation devices with safety harness should be worn at all times. A Lifesaver buoy and line are kept on the stern rail. A boarding ladder is kept in the cockpit locker for attachment to the gate stanchions on either side of the boat. Jacklines are provided for operation of the boat in rough weather. It is recommended that they should always be used for night passages. It is recommended that a tackle, at least four-part, is kept stowed on the lifelines amidships during passages for attachment to a halyard to enable a man overboard to be hoisted from the sea. 

A liferaft cradle for a hard canister is fitted on top of the main hatch cover.

Engine parts should be given a safe berth during operation. 

The propellor is an area of extreme danger when the engine is running.

Navigation lights are operated from the panel by the navigation desk.

Areas in which combustible fuels are being burned should be properly ventilated at all times.

All equipment should be properly stowed when the boat is under way. Working areas, particularly the cockpit and foredeck, should be kept tidy and free of clutter.

The toilet empties solely into the holding tank. It can not be emptied direct to sea. The holding tank may be pumped out to sea by switching the Y-valve and operating the hand-pump located in the head sink-unit, or via the starboard deck pump-out deck-plate.
